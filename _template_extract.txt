ABSTRACT 
TITLE: AI-Based Scholarship and Internship Candidate Selection System 
Name of Researcher: NTARINDWA Nshuti Allan 
Faculty Advisor: RUGWIZA Jean  Eric 
Date Completed: June, 2026 
The fair and transparent selection of candidates for scholarships and internships remains a 
significant challenge for many organizations in Rwanda. Mastery Hub of Rwanda, an organization 
dedicated to student development, currently manages candidate selecti on through manual 
processes involving paper applications, spreadsheet tracking, and uncoordinated committee 
discussions. This approach has created substantial challenges including lack of transparency, 
delays in selection decisions, difficulty in comparing  candidates objectively, limited access to 
reliable information, and potential for subjective bias. 
This project developed an AI -Based Scholarship and Internship Candidate Selection System to 
address these challenges by providing a centralized, intelligent digital platform for managing the 
complete candidate selection lifecycle. The system enables studen ts to create comprehensive 
profiles, submit applications with supporting documents, and track application progress in real 
time. Internally, the system supports structured application intake, automated eligibility 
verification, AI -driven candidate scoring and ranking, intelligent opportunity matching, 
asynchronous committee discussion forums, digital voting, and fairness reporting. 
The system was implemented using a modern technology stack with the frontend developed in 
React.js, the backend built using Spring Boot (Java), and PostgreSQL for relational data storage. 
Artificial intelligence capabilities were integrated using Python -based libraries including Scikit -
learn for candidate scoring algorithms. Additional integrations include JWT and Google OAuth2 
for secure authentication, Twilio API for SMS notifications, and Gmail SMTP for email 
communication.

GENERAL INTRODUCTION 
INTRODUCTION  
Mastery Hub of Rwanda is an innovative organization that supports student development through 
scholarship and internship programs in Kigali. Established to create educational and professional 
opportunities for Rwandan students, the organization partners wi th educational institutions and 
corporate sponsors to provide valuable learning and career experiences. Its mission is to equip 
students with the skills and support needed to succeed in Rwanda's evolving economy. 
A critical but often neglected aspect of student support is the fair and transparent selection of 
candidates for limited opportunities. The process of evaluating qualifications, comparing 
applicants, and matching candidates to programs must be conducted wi th integrity. Fairness is 
essential for maintaining trust among applicants, donors, and partners. Without transparency, 
organizations risk losing credibility and overlooking deserving candidates. However, the current 
process at Mastery Hub is manual, fragm ented, and opaque. This creates inefficiencies including 
delayed decisions, inconsistent evaluation, lost documents, limited feedback, and increased staff 
workload. 
The AI -Based Scholarship and Internship Candidate Selection System is a web application 
developed to digitize and automate the selection lifecycle. It transitions from a paper -based 
workflow to a centralized digital platform with artificial intelligence ca pabilities. The system 
provides real -time transparency, automated candidate evaluation, AI -driven scoring, and data -
driven analytics. By leveraging AI, the system reduces human bias, increases processing speed, 
and ensures fair consideration based on objec tive criteria. It analyzes academic performance, 
extracurricular achievements, leadership experience, and program fit. The system augments rather 
than replaces human judgment, providing insights for informed decisions. This system represents 
a significant step forward in the digital transformation of student support services in Rwanda.

BACKGROUND OF THE STUDY

organizations operate, compete, and deliver value in the education and professional development 
sectors. In Rwanda, access to scholarships and internships plays a critical ro le in shaping the

enhance their academic and professional prospects. As the demand for these opportunities grows,

scholarship and internship beneficiaries. These approaches are often static, error-prone, and unable 
to provide real-time insights into candidate qualifications and program fit. As a result, selection

candidates, and ensuring that selection decisions are free from bias. 
This challenge is evident at Mastery Hub of Rwanda, an organization dedicated to supporting 
student development through scholarship and internship programs in Kigali. As Mastery Hub 
expands its program portfolio and receives increasing numbers of applications, the complexity of 
managing candidate selection processes increases. Without a centralized and intelligent selection 
system, it becomes difficult to align candidate profiles with program requirements, leading to 
inefficiencies in resource allocation and potential dissatisfaction among applicants.

STATEMENT OF THE PROBLEM 
Organizations offering scholarships and internships increasingly operate in competitive 
environments where the quality and fairness of selection processes directly impact their reputation 
and effectiveness. However, many organizations lack an effective and  intelligent mechanism to 
accurately evaluate candidate profiles, identify the most suitable applicants, and ensure 
transparency in selection decisions. 
In the context of Mastery Hub of Rwanda, an organization providing scholarship and internship 
opportunities to students, the challenge is particularly evident. As the organization receives diverse 
applications from students across multiple institutions, ca ndidate qualifications span various 
academic disciplines, extracurricular achievements, and personal backgrounds. Currently, 
candidate selection and program matching are largely dependent on manual records, fragmented 
tools, and subjective assessments. The se approaches do not provide real -time visibility into 
applicant qualifications nor support systematic analysis of candidate-program fit. 
The impact of this problem includes inefficient selection processes, delayed program placements, 
underutilization of available opportunities, and poorly matched candidate -program assignments. 
Without accurate candidate evaluation, deserving students may be  overlooked, leading to missed 
educational and professional development opportunities. Additionally, the inability to quickly 
identify suitable candidates for specific programs limits organizational agility and scalability. 
The root cause of these challenges lies in the absence of a centralized, data-driven, and intelligent 
system capable of integrating applicant data, analyzing candidate qualifications, and supporting 
informed selection decisions. The lack of analytical and AI-based tools further prevents objective 
evaluation and fairness monitoring. Addressing this problem is essential for Mastery Hub of 
Rwanda to optimize its selection processes, enhance program effectiveness, and maintain trust 
among applicants and stakeholders.

and Internship Candidate Selection System that digitizes and automates the full lifecycle of 
candidate selection at Mastery Hub of Rwanda, enabling efficient application management,

 METHODOLOGY AND TECHNIQUES 
This study employs appropriate research methods to identify and analyze existing weaknesses in 
the scholarship and internship candidate selection process and to design and develop a new AI -
Based Scholarship and Internship Candidate Selection System by leveraging modern information 
technology solutions. These methods were selected to ensure accurate data collection, reliable 
analysis, and practical system design. The following methods and techniques were applied in 
conducting this research: 
Documentation 
Documentation is a research method that involves the systematic collection, review, and analysis 
of existing records, reports, and written materials relevant to the study topic. It helps researchers 
understand the background information, identify operation al challenges, and gather evidence to 
support research objectives. 
In this study, I used documentation review to examine existing records, reports, and policies from 
Mastery Hub of Rwanda. This included analyzing current application forms, selection guidelines, 
evaluation rubrics, committee meeting minutes, decision notification templates, and past applicant 
records. The review also covered digital transformation policies such as the Smart Rwanda Master 
Plan and the National Strategy for Transformation (NST2) to align the system's objectives with 
national priorities. Additionally, I explored academic publications and international best practices 
on AI -driven candidate selection systems, scholarship management platforms, and fairness 
monitoring in automated decision -making to guide the system's design and functionality (Marr , 
2021; Mehrabi et al., 2021). 
Interview Questions and Responses 
Interviews are a qualitative research method involving structured or semi -structured discussions 
with key stakeholders to obtain detailed insights into their experiences, perceptions, and 
operational challenges. This method provides practical data that com plement findings from 
observation and documentation. 
In this study, I conducted interviews with program managers, committee members, and student 
applicants from Mastery Hub of Rwanda. These discussions provided an in -depth understanding 
of the manual candidate selection process and highlighted the need for a  more transparent,

Below are the key questions asked during the interviews, along with summarized responses: 
Question 1: How do students currently submit their scholarship and internship applications, and 
what challenges do you encounter during the intake process? 
Response: "Students submit their applications either physically at our office or by sending emails 
to a shared mailbox. The problem is that there is no standardized form, so submissions arrive in 
different formats and with varying levels of completeness. We then ha ve to follow up manually, 
which takes a lot of time and delays the registration process." 
Question 2: How do you currently evaluate and compare candidates for opportunities? 
Response: "We use a spreadsheet where reviewers enter scores for different criteria. However, not 
everyone scores consistently. One reviewer might rate a student's leadership experience highly 
while another gives it low marks. There is no standardized rubric, so th e final ranking often 
depends on who is in the room." 
Question 3: How are supporting documents managed throughout the application process? 
Response: "Documents are stored in different places. Physical submissions are kept in filing 
cabinets, while email attachments remain in inboxes. There is no central repository. I have seen 
situations where transcripts get lost because different staff members handl e different parts of the 
same application without proper coordination." 
Question 4: How are committee decisions recorded, and do you believe the current method is 
reliable?

Observation 
Direct observation sessions were carried out within Mastery Hub of Rwanda over a period of two 
weeks during an academic internship. The researcher observed daily operational activities across 
===== PAGE 22 =====
10 
 
the full candidate selection workflow, from initial application intake through committee 
deliberation and final decision notification. The following findings were documented through 
direct observation: 
1. Manual and Fragmented Application Registration 
It was observed that students arriving at Mastery Hub office were required to fill out paper 
application forms and submit physical documents. In several instances, staff members had to 
request additional documents from students because the submission was i ncomplete, resulting in 
repeated visits. Email submissions arrived in an unstructured shared inbox with no automated 
acknowledgment, and these were manually copied into a spreadsheet register by a program 
manager. I observed that on some occasions, newly received emails remained unread for an entire 
day before being processed, directly contributing to registration delays. 
2. Disconnected Document Storage Across Multiple Locations 
Supporting documents for a single application were observed to exist in multiple disconnected 
locations simultaneously. Physical copies were stored in labeled folders in filing cabinets, while 
electronic attachments from email submissions remained in indiv idual staff members' personal 
inboxes. Academic transcripts and recommendation letters were saved on local hard drives. I 
observed instances where a program manager spent considerable time searching through physical 
files and email threads to assemble all documents for an application before being able to begin 
evaluation. This fragmentation made it difficult to confirm that all required materials had been 
collected, and three applications were observed where referenced documents could not be 
immediately located. 
3. Inefficient Committee Coordination 
During the observation period, the researcher directly witnessed a committee deliberation 
scheduling process. The Committee Leader composed and sent an email to five committee 
members proposing three possible meeting dates. Over the following three days, responses arrived 
gradually and asynchronously, with some members not responding at all until followed up by 
phone. Application materials, including student profiles, academic records, and supporting 
documents, were compiled and attached to a separate email  distributed the evening before the 
===== PAGE 23 =====
11 
 
scheduled meeting. One member indicated during the session that they had not had time to review 
all applications, which affected the quality of deliberation for two candidates. 
4. Informal and Unverifiable Voting Records 
Committee decisions observed during the internship were recorded by a secretary who took 
handwritten notes during the meeting. Votes were expressed verbally by each member, and no 
individual vote record was created separately from the minutes. I observed t hat when a vote 
resulted in a split outcome, there was a brief verbal disagreement over the correct count, and the 
secretary had to ask each member to restate their decision. There was no mechanism to verify 
quorum independently of the minutes, nor to produce an auditable per-member voting record after 
the fact. 
5. Absence of Real-Time Reporting and Management Visibility 
When senior management requested a summary of current application volumes and selection rates, 
I observed that a program manager spent approximately half a working day compiling data 
manually from the spreadsheet register, physical files, and email records . The resulting report 
contained data that was several days out of date and acknowledged gaps where information could 
not be confirmed. This confirmed the absence of any centralized reporting mechanism and 
demonstrated the direct cost of the manual approac h in terms of staff time and decision -making 
quality. 
Collectively, these observations confirmed the operational challenges described by interviewees 
and provided concrete, firsthand evidence of the inefficiencies that the proposed AI -Based 
Scholarship and Internship Candidate Selection System was designed to address. The observation 
method ensured that the system requirements were grounded in real institutional practice rather 
than theoretical assumptions alone.

DESCRIPTION OF CURRENT SYSTEM 
The existing candidate selection process at Mastery Hub of Rwanda is conducted entirely through 
a cumbersome combination of physical document handling, disjointed email correspondence, and 
manual record-keeping methods that have remained largely unchanged for years. When a student 
wishes to apply for a scholarship or internship opportunity, they must initiate the entire process by 
submitting a formal application form, either in person by visiting the Mastery Hub office during 
operating hours or by email to a designated organizational address that is monitored intermittently. 
This lack of a centralized digital portal means applicants have no real-time visibility into the status 
of their submissions. 
Upon receipt of an application, a staff member typically a Program Manager must manually enter 
the applicant's personal details, academic information, and supporting documentation into either a 
basic spreadsheet or a physical paper register stored in a filing cabinet. This manual data entry 
process is time-consuming, error-prone, and offers no automated validation or duplicate detection. 
The application is then reviewed subjectively to determine whether it meets the formal 
requirements for a valid submissio n, a step that relies heavily on the individual judgment of the 
staff member rather than any standardized checklist or automated eligibility engine. If the 
application is accepted, it is forwarded to a Program Manager who bears the responsibility of 
evaluating the candidate against specific program criteria, carefully reviewing academic records 
line by line, and assessing supporting documents such as transcripts, recommendation letters, and 
personal statements all without any analytical tools to assist in comparison. 
When applications have been preliminarily reviewed and are ready for committee consideration, 
the relevant Committee Leader must coordinate schedules manually, typically by circulating 
multiple emails back and forth to identify a suitable meeting time that accommodates all members. 
This scheduling process alone can take several days or even weeks. Application documents are 
then prepared, printed or attached, and distributed individually by email to each committee 
member. During the actual committee meeting, members discuss each candidate orally and attempt 
to reach a collective decision through unstructured conversation, which is then recorded manually 
in meeting minutes that are often incomplete or difficult to reference later. This entire workflow 
lacks tr ansparency, efficiency, and standardization, ultimately compromising the fairness and 
timeliness of selection outcomes.

Strengths of the Current System 
Despite its significant limitations, the existing system has several genuine strengths that must be 
acknowledged. 
• Low initial cost: The system requires no specialized software investment and can operate 
with standard office tools available to all Mastery Hub staff, including basic computers and 
internet access. 
• Familiarity: Staff are accustomed to the current process and do not require training on 
new technologies, which reduces the immediate burden of learning entirely new 
workflows. 
• Flexibility: Manual handling allows staff to manage unusual or exceptional applications 
without being constrained by rigid system logic, accommodating special circumstances 
that automated systems might reject. 
• No technology dependency:  The process can continue during system outages, network 
failures, or other technical disruptions, ensuring that selection activities are never 
completely halted. 
• Personal communication:  Direct contact between staff and students can facilitate 
clarification of application requirements and build institutional relationships that 
encourage future applications. 
Weaknesses of the Current System 
The weaknesses of the current system are extensive and serious. They can be organized into four 
categories: tracking and transparency failures, documentation fragmentation, governance 
deficiencies, and analytical limitations. 
Tracking and Transparency Failures 
The most visible weakness of the current system is the complete absence of real-time tracking for 
applicants. Once an application is submitted, the student has no automated way to determine its

Documentation Fragmentation 
Application materials associated with each submission are stored in multiple disconnected

decision is ever challenged, the organization lacks the documented evidence needed to defend its 
process. This governance gap exposes Mastery Hub to reputational risk and potential disputes with 
applicants, donors, or partner institutions.

UNIFIED MODELING LANGUAGE (UML DESIGN) 
Unified Modeling Language (UML) is the internationally standardized notation for modeling 
software systems. It provides a family of diagram types each suited to capturing a different aspect 
of system behavior or structure  that together provide a complete and unambiguous specification 
of how the system is designed to work. In this project, UML serves three purposes: it guides 
development by providing a precise blueprint for implementation; it communicates system design 
to stakeholders who may not be technic al specialists; and it forms part of the permanent 
documentation of the system. 
 Design of the New System Diagrams 
The design process determines how the functionalities specified in the analysis model will be 
implemented. It has the purpose of creating a technical solution that satisfies the functional 
requirements for the system. 
Use Case Diagram 
Use case diagrams are visual tools within UML that show the interactions between a system and 
its external environment, capturing the essential business requirements for system operation. These 
diagrams represent a business entity or software system, its e xternal stakeholders (known as 
actors), and a set of tasks (use cases) that users are expected or authorized to perform when 
interacting with the system. 
These diagrams utilize the following symbols: 
Actor 
When directly interacting with a system, an external entity takes on a designated role defined by 
an actor. This role might represent a user's function or a role fulfilled by another system that 
engages with the given system

29 
 
Use Case 
The use case entails detailing the sequence of actions that a system can undertake while interacting 
with external actors. It encompasses tasks that the system should perform in response to an actor's 
request. 
Relationship 
Genuine associations illustrate the direct interactions between actors and use cases in a system. 
These are represented using the UML association symbol, indicating a meaningful connection 
within the system's functionality. This helps ensure that all user interactions are clearly defined 
and properly linked to the system's operations. 
 
System Boundary 
A box is drawn around the use case diagram to visually represent the system's boundary. This 
defines the scope of the modeled system and distinguishes internal functionalities from external 
interactions. It helps stakeholders clearly identify what is included within the system and what lies

Class Diagrams 
A class diagram serves as a fundamental component of software modeling and design in the field 
of software engineering. It provides a visual representation of the building blocks of an application, 
aiding in the understanding and planning of software syste ms. Within a class diagram, each class 
typically consists of three key elements: attributes, operations (methods), and associations. 
Attributes represent the properties or data members of a class, defining the state that an object of 
that class can hold. Operations, also known as methods, define the behaviors or functions that the 
class can perform, specifying what actions an object can execute when requested. Associations 
illustrate the relationships and connections between classes, allowing designers to d epict how 
different classes interact within the system and influence one another's behavior. These 
relationships can include inheritance (is -a), association (has -a), aggregation (part -of), and 
composition (stronger part-of), each represented by specific UM L notation. Through these three 
elements, a class diagram captures both the data structure and behavioral responsibilities of system 
components, making it an indispensable tool for object -oriented analysis and design. Without a 
well-constructed class diagr am, developers risk creating systems with redundant code, unclear 
responsibilities, or inconsistent data relationships, leading to maintenance difficulties and reduced 
software quality. 
In UML, the Unified Modeling Language, class diagrams play a crucial role in modeling the static 
structure of a software system. They not only reveal the relationships between classes but also 
showcase source code dependencies, inheritance hierarchies, and interface implementations. This 
makes class diagrams an indispensable tool for both high-level architectural design and low-level 
coding details, bridging the gap between abstract design and concrete implementation while 
ensuring consistency and traceability throughout the development process.

Activity Diagrams 
An activity diagram represents the behavior of a business process by showing the main tasks and 
activities involved from beginning to end. It can model anything from high-level workflows across 
multiple use cases to the detailed steps of a single method or  function. Every activity diagram 
begins with a start node and ends with a final node, ensuring a clear flow from initiation to 
completion. 
These diagrams are especially useful for visualizing the flow of control and the logical sequence 
of tasks. They include actions, decisions, parallel activities, and outcomes, making complex 
processes significantly easier to understand and communicate. Sta ndard UML notations, such as 
circles for start and end nodes, diamonds for decision points, and arrows for directional flow, make 
them simple and consistent to read across different projects. 
The flexibility of activity diagrams makes them valuable to both managers and developers. At a 
high level, they show overall workflows for stakeholders and business leaders, while at a detailed 
level, they guide developers through the step -by-step execution of a use case. For example, the 
activity diagram presents the complete workflow of the AI -Based Scholarship and Internship 
Candidate Selection System from the perspective of all actors, showing how the system progresses 
through its defined states and where decision points determine the flow of control. 
In the context of this project, the activity diagram begins when a student submits an application 
through the online portal. The diagram then branches based on eligibility verification, with 
ineligible applications being rejected immediately while eligible  ones proceed to AI scoring and 
ranking. Subsequent decision points determine whether a reviewer is assigned, whether the 
committee approves or rejects the candidate through digital voting, and whether the final outcome 
is communicated to the student via a utomated notification. This visual representation helps 
stakeholders understand the entire selection logic at a glance without needing to read through 
lengthy technical documentation.

Database Diagrams 
The database is implemented in PostgreSQL and designed in strict accordance with Third Normal 
Form (3NF) to minimize data redundancy, eliminate duplicate information, and ensure complete 
referential integrity across all tables. The schema comprises multipl e core tables organized 
logically around the central Student, Application, and Program entities, with additional supporting 
tables for related data such as reviews, votes, notifications, and audit logs. In the context of the 
AI-Based Scholarship and Intern ship Candidate Selection System developed specifically for 
Mastery Hub of Rwanda, the database design must accommodate complex and interconnected data 
requirements including student profiles, academic records, program definitions, application 
submissions, AI scoring results, reviewer evaluations, committee discussions, voting records, final 
decisions, and comprehensive audit trails for compliance purposes. The design must also support 
role-based access control to restrict data visibility based on user roles, ensure data security through 
encryption and proper authentication mechanisms, and maintain complete historical records for 
long-term compliance, reporting, and future analysis needs. 
To achieve these objectives, the database implements foreign key constraints to maintain 
relationships between tables, indexing strategies to optimize query performance during peak 
application periods, and timestamp columns to track when each record was cr eated or modified. 
Separate tables are dedicated to storing AI-generated scores and their contributing factors, enabling 
explainable AI outputs that show why a particular candidate received a specific ranking. 
Additionally, the audit log table captures every significant action in a read-only format, recording 
the user identifier, timestamp, action type, and affected record, which provides a tamper -evident 
trail that can be reviewed during disputes or external audits. This thoughtful design ensures that 
the database not only meets current operational needs but also scales effectively as Mastery Hub 
expands its program offerings and user base in future years.

System Architecture Design 
The system is built on a layered architecture that provides clear separation of concerns, 
independent scalability, and maintainability. The architecture consists of five layers: Presentation 
Layer, Application Layer, AI Service Layer, Data Access Layer, and Data Storage Layer. 
System Architecture Diagram 
Figure 9: System Architecture Diagram 

===== PAGE 68 =====
56 
 
• Presentation Layer (React.js/Next.js): Manages all user interface rendering and client -
side interactions. This layer includes the Student Portal for applicants, Program Manager 
Dashboard for administrators, Reviewer Dashboard for evaluators, Committee Workspace 
for collaborative discussion, and Admin Panel for system oversight. 
• Application Layer (Spring Boot REST API): Contains the core business logic that drives 
the entire system. This layer is composed of Authentication Controllers for secure login, 
Profile Controllers for user management, Application Controllers for submission handling, 
Program Controllers for opport unity configuration, Review Controllers for evaluation 
management, Vote Controllers for digital decision recording, and Report Controllers for 
analytics generation 
• AI Service Layer (Python):  Handles all artificial intelligence and machine learning 
functionality. This layer includes the Eligibility Verification Service for automated 
candidate screening, Candidate Scoring Engine for intelligent ranking, Opportunity 
Matching Algorithm for aligning students with suitable programs, and Fairness Monitoring 
Service for detecting potential bias in selection outcomes. 
• Data Access Layer (Spring Data JPA): Provides repository interfaces and query methods 
that bridge the application logic with the underlying database, enabling seamless data 
retrieval and persistence operations. 
• Data Storage Layer (PostgreSQL + Cloud Storage): Stores all structured data including 
student profiles, applications, scores, and voting records in PostgreSQL, while uploaded 
documents such as transcripts and recommendation letters are stored in cloud storage. 
• External Services : Integrates with third -party APIs including Twilio for SMS 
notifications, Gmail SMTP for email communications, and Google OAuth2 for secure 
social authentication.

SOFTWARE TESTING 
The design of software benefits from the use of software tests. They aid in determining whether the 
software genuinely accomplishes the task it was designed to tackle. 
When undertaking software testing, it is vital to keep the following things in mind: 
• Is the application compliant with the standards that guided its design and development? 
• Is the application functioning as it should? 
• Is it possible to implement the application in the same way and satisfy the needs of the 
stakeholders? 
The following are some software testing techniques: 
The Unit Test 
Unit testing is a technique for ensuring the correct operation of a particular piece of software or a 
program. It is a method of evaluating the acceptability for use of individual pieces of source code, 
sets of one or more computer program modules, related control data, usage processes, and operating 
procedures. In other words, every little component that can be assembled with the aim of verifying 
that each part is in accordance with its specifications and checking for logical errors. Unit testing is 
a powerful tool that allows for the most thorough error detection. At every point where code has 
been created, a unit test has been run on the application. For the AI-Based Scholarship and Internship 
Candidate Selection System, unit tests were conducted on criti cal components including the 
eligibility verification module, AI scoring algorithm, application submission handler, and 
notification service. Each method was tested in isolation with both positive and negative test cases 
to ensure correct functionality. 
The Integration Test 
Integration testing is the stage of software testing where many software components are combined 
and tested together. This test is crucial for confirming the correct assembly of the software's 
numerous components. As more tests are run, the hardware and software components are gathered 
and tested, and eventually the full system is tested. The application modules were thoroughly tested 
one after another until they were finished to guarantee that the assembled software components met 
all of the necessary func tional and technical requirements. For this system, integration testing 
===== PAGE 103 =====
91 
 
focused on the interaction between the frontend and backend APIs, the communication between the 
application layer and the AI service layer, and the data flow between the application and the 
PostgreSQL database. The integration of external services including Twilio SMS, Gmail SMTP, and 
Google OAuth2 was also verified to ensure seamless operation. 
The Validation Test 
The software is validated in its external context during the final test step. To guarantee that it fully 
satisfies the requirements established in the beginning phase, the product has been tested in its final 
configuration. The validation test is essential  to make sure that the setup of the application 
corresponds to the needs stated. For instance, the student ID must be unique, the email address must

thoroughly tested, and it was during this process that we discovered that the operations' progress 
matched the functional requirements. User acceptance testing was conducted with program 
managers, reviewers, and student applicants from Mastery Hub of Rwanda to validate that the system 
meets their expectations and operational needs. The validation test confirmed that the system

program managers to grant or deny scholarships and internships

CONCLUSIONS AND RECOMMENDATIONS 
 CONCLUSIONS 
The AI-Based Scholarship and Internship Candidate Selection System developed in this study 
successfully addresses the identified problems in manual candidate selection processes at Mastery 
Hub of Rwanda. The system provides a centralized, intelligent, and transparent platform that 
supports efficient and fair selection of scholarship and internship beneficiaries. 
The system demonstrates that AI can be effectively applied to candidate selection in the Rwandan 
context, improving efficiency, transparency, and fairness while maintaining human oversight of 
final decisions. The layered architecture and use of industry-standard technologies ensure that the 
system is maintainable and scalable for future growth. 
All specific objectives established in Chapter One were achieved. A secure, wizard-based student 
portal was designed and implemented, enabling online profile creation, application submission, 
and real-time status tracking. An automated eligibility verifica tion module was developed that 
checks candidate qualifications against program requirements instantly. An AI-powered candidate 
scoring and ranking engine was implemented using configurable criteria weighting. An intelligent 
opportunity matching system was built to recommend suitable programs to students. A fairness 
monitoring dashboard was created providing management with real-time metrics on selection rates 
and demographic distributions. External communication services including Twilio SMS and 
Gmail SMTP were integrated for automated notifications. Role -based access control using JWT 
and Google OAuth2 was implemented ensuring appropriate access for each user role. 
The system provides a transparent, efficient, and auditable platform for the management of 
scholarship and internship candidate selection that is well -suited to the operational realities of 
Mastery Hub of Rwanda and aligned with Rwanda's national strategy for digital transformation 
and innovation.

RECOMMENDATIONS 
Based on the findings and limitations of this study, the following recommendations are offered. 
Mobile Application Development 
While the web application is responsive, a dedicated mobile application would improve 
accessibility for students whose primary internet access is through smartphones. A Progressive 
Web App approach could provide offline capabilities for profile creation and application drafting, 
meaningfully expanding reach, particularly for students outside Kigali. 
Advanced Natural Language Processing 
Future versions should incorporate more sophisticated NLP techniques to analyze personal 
statements and essays. Sentiment analysis, topic modeling, and writing quality assessment could 
provide additional scoring dimensions beyond structured criteria, addin g nuance to candidate 
evaluation. 
Integration with Educational Databases 
Integration with partner institution databases would enable automatic verification of academic 
records, reducing reliance on self-reported data and uploaded documents. This would require API 
development and data sharing agreements with partner institutions. 
Predictive Analytics for Program Success 
With sufficient historical data, predictive models could estimate the likelihood of candidate 
success in scholarship or internship programs. Such models would inform selection decisions and 
help identify candidates who would benefit most from specific opportunities. 
Explainable AI Enhancements 
While the current system provides score breakdowns, more detailed explanations of AI decisions 
would increase user trust and understanding. Techniques such as LIME (Local Interpretable 
Model-agnostic Explanations) could be integrated to generate natural la nguage explanations of 
scoring outcomes.
